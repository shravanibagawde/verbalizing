Notes on Verbalizing is written, designed and programmed by Shravani Bagawde.

This is a programmed transcript of a showcase originally performed on April 12, 2024, at 2 W 13th Street, New York, NY, for an approximate duration of 8 minutes.
I figured I’d start by talking about a place I have become extremely acquainted with in these past few years:
A textEdit file on my laptop, lovingly titled SLOP.
SLOP has become a repository for every inkling of a project that has come to me these past few years.

When we began this semester, I frequently consulted the file in hopes of finding some viable direction for my thesis...
...translating its ideas on 11"x17" sheets of copy paper that were folded into fourths.
These ideas were almost always rooted in some sort of verbose research topic, like internet infrastructure or neoliberal digitality.

To me, design was ultimately an avenue for the research and writing I was more eager to do.
I would scan each of these ideas and ask myself: wouldn’t these just be better off as essays?

Does every exploration really need a designed intervention?
My comfortable thesis would have been something analytical with a clear framework, and fluidly typeset once I was all done —a practice I was fairly confident with.
For our first thesis assignment, we were asked to make a book out of 256 images as our first assignment. I took it as an opportunity to finalize and publish a paper I had written about WhatsApp image sharing cultures.
(See full project)
When I finally sat down to write the critical essays that would form the backbone of my thesis, I almost immediately hit a wall.

Ideas that once seemed so generative in their clipped forms suddenly seemed dubious and difficult to expand into fully-fledged texts.
Put simply, I didn’t know what to write.
With nowhere else to go, I decided to investigate this very feeling, and interrogate what exactly it is to doubt.
I tried to gather as many answers to this question, by examining my own notes...
...looking through existing texts...
(See Disaster Media, by Lisa Parks & Janet Walker)
...and asking people around me.
I began documenting these theories through a set of threaded web pages.
Some described doubt as a kind of perpetual swelling...
...some saw it as something conspiratorial...
...and some described it as a willingness to change.
This project is called ✼ Querying and over time, it became a sort of futile search engine...
...one that would only answer a single question and then trap you into a series of uncertain answers.
I was fascinated by these small instances of instability, and figuring out how they could be visually represented.

User-uploaded Google Maps views turned to be a great interface to capture and appropriate such moments.
Documentation mishaps would result in phantom-like insertions of these users’ bodies.
I kept collecting these locations in a spreadsheet, recording them to create ✼ Glitching, a montage dedicated to archiving the accidental and erroneous.
By now, having pretty much veered off from my original goal of developing succinct essays, I began writing out snippets of this meandering process I found myself in...
...on a small site aptly named ✼ Logging.
With these short reflections and conversations with those around me, I recognized that it was this very act of quickly articulating that I had been interested in all along.

Perhaps the fragmented ideas in my textEdit file SLOP could be more compelling if they weren’t forced to be scaled up and instead, allowed to just exist in fragments.
Or as historian Susan Buck-Morss calls them, “pieces of text that transform themselves... like folding chairs and folding beds." (See Seeing ↔︎ Making: Room for Thought, by Kevin McCaughey, Adam Michaels & Susan Buck-Morss)
I then began to think about the invisible footprints and unoccupied space between these fragments.
What can be gleaned from gaps? What is the difference between finding gaps and making gaps?
I gathered various thoughts on absences, set them as a block of text on another web page, and created three levels of subtraction.
Drawing from existing practices of concrete poetry and redaction, ✼ Voiding explores the formal and textual experiences constructed from the singular gesture of removal.
With a default print-on-demand mechanism, I’ve been able to crop and collate the many iterations of gaps created by people playing with this tool.
These attempts to understand and graphically translate “Voids”, “Glitches”, or “Queries” eventually brought me to the term verbalizing...
...a continuous verb conveying the process of turning something abstract into something tangible and public.
It is an activity that uses words as its most basic material, and blurs the lines between articulation and form-making.
From this, I wanted to examine how words could exist as ornaments, as formal motifs serving a decorative purpose and incidentally conveying information.
✼ Detailing is a digital frame built out of notes debating if the ornament should be seen a carrier of meaning or if it can simply be prized for its pleasurable effect.
This frame is also made up of other “superfluous elements” like hyperlinked dingbats, scanned borders from archived books and crowd-sourced accounts of “pleasurable details” that are difficult to rationalize.
The frame is manually coded, embellished segment by segment, a process that feels synonymous with some of the decorative crafts embedded within its links.
✼ Detailing is a piece that is in progress, with a frame that slowly encroaches the browser’s edges with each new addition.
A second definition of “verbalizing” describes the process of turning a noun into a verb.
Emplaced objects become mobile and continuous actions. The designer or author undermines their control and privileges participation, pluralism, and context.
I began compiling an index called “Citing”, a pop-up that serves as a bibliography, reading list and acknowledgement of the people that led me to specific fragments or ideas at large.
All fragments I collected thus far were sourced and stored through specific media formats, creating generative constraints for their documentation.
For instance, using voicemail to collect responses for ✼ Querying afforded me to transcribe the hesitations and uncertainties people expressed when defining the experience of doubt.
Something else that I valued through this process was flatness.
By using open source typefaces, black ink on standard-sizes of copy paper, and mediums like spreadsheets or email, I felt like I could leverage existing systems to quickly reproduce, circulate and collaborate on these works
Flatness, fragmentation and other attributes are examined in a kind of meta notes reader, printed with a risograph on 11"x17" sheets of copy paper that are folded into fourths.

A third interpretion of "verbalizing" is a tendency to excessively speak.
This isn't a self-deprecating or existential provocation, but more or less a moment to reflect.
Despite all this talk of ambiguity and fragmentation, I'm still reading from a pre-written script, and planning how all of this could be packaged, adapted, and archived.
After many attempts at styling this thesis as a modular syllabus, or an interpretive toolkit, I've come to recognize it as an opportunity.
An window to make sense of things in a meandering way, to exchange with around me, and to create —not with a defined outcome— but a gentle continous momentum.
At a time where fields like design and writing find themselves entrenched in precarity and hyperindividualism, I'm pretty uncertain as to when another chance like this might come.
But I think there is value to be found in writing, designing, researching with no immediate productive outcome.
The ideas in SLOP don’t have to be works pressing to be fully constructed. They could be content with their unpolished form, shifting with discussions and shaped by relationships.
And so lastly I wonder, how do we find the time, spacem and resources to Verbalize? What does someone else's Verbalizing look like?

I don't have immediate answers to these questions, but I am keen on continuing this conversation...
